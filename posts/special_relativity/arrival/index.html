On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies

In this two-part series, we will reproduce Einstein's 1905 papers on special relativity. 1. Introduction

Consider a conductor and a magnet. If the magnet is in motion while the conductor is stationary, an electric field is induced. But if the conductor moves while the magnet is stationary, the phenomenon is explained by the Lorentz force acting on charges. The observable effect only depends on the relative motion, but the explanations draw a sharp distinction dependent on the observer.

Moreover, attempts to detect absolute motion through the ether all failed. Arago’s null result, the Fizeau experiment, and the Michelson-Morley result each insisted that no preferred frame could be found.

It is from this tension that we are led to a new framework. Rather than inventing mechanical hypotheses to patch inconsistencies, we propose to retain only those principles which withstand all scrutiny and discard the rest. In their place, we introduce two postulates from which the observed phenomena follow naturally.

2. The Postulates

Postulate I: Principle of Relativity. The same laws of electrodynamics and optics will be valid for all frames of reference for which the equations of mechanics hold good.

Postulate II: Constancy of the Speed of Light. Light is always propagated in empty space with a definite velocity ccc which is independent of the state of motion of the emitting body.

4. Physical Consequences of the Transformation

4.1 The Relativity of Simultaneity

The immediate physical consequence suggested by the transformation is the relativity of simultaneity. Due to the t′=γ(t−vc2x)t' = \gamma(t - \frac{v}{c^2}x)t′=γ(t−c2v​x) transformation, two events happening simultaneously in one frame are not simultaneous in another frame if v≠0v\neq 0v=0. Due to the relativity of simultaneity, we need to treat time of different frames carefully. When we talk about the time, it is the time of a specific inertial frame, or if you prefer a more tangible description, the time read by a stationary clock relative to the frame. We assume that we can synchronize all the clocks of an inertial frame, and they will remain synchronized.
