1. The Moving Stars

Measuring Parallax

The Law of Sines

sin⁡αa=sin⁡βb\frac{\sin\alpha}{a} = \frac{\sin\beta}{b}asinα​=bsinβ​

Declination (Dec): Equivalent to a polar angle in spherical coordinates. Dec is measured in degrees, where the celestial equator has a declination of 0∘0^\circ0∘, and the North celestial pole has a declination of 90∘90^\circ90∘. Due to the Earth's axial tilt of 23.4∘23.4^\circ23.4∘, the Sun's declination varies from +23.4∘+23.4^\circ+23.4∘ (the June solstice) to −23.4∘-23.4^\circ−23.4∘ (the December solstice).

In the 17th century, one decisive experiment to verify the Copernican theory of the solar system was to detect stellar parallax. As the Earth orbits around the Sun, an observer on the Earth sees an apparent shift of the star's direction. One particular experiment is to observe the apparent changes in a star's declination, the angular distance of a point north or south of the celestial equator.

Also note that the angular shift has a sinusoidal pattern due to the change in ϕ\phiϕ and α\alphaα, and the extrema are obtained 6 months apart.

In 1669, Hooke set out to observe Gamma Draconis (GD), a bright star that passes nearly directly overhead of London, and claimed to have observed the maximal parallax of 30′′30''30′′. Now we know that Hooke interpreted the data incorrectly, and the true parallax of GD is about 0.02′′0.02''0.02′′. What's crucially wrong is not about the magnitude of the measurements though. What Hooke missed was that the pattern was 90 degrees out of phase: the extrema occur in the wrong seasons.

Right ascension (RA): Equivalent to an azimuth angle in a spherical coordinate system. The zero points are in the direction of the Sun from Earth at the March equinox. RA is measured in hours eastward corresponding to the Earth's orbital direction (counterclockwise viewed from the North celestial pole), where a full cycle corresponds to 24 hours. Therefore, the Earth is at 12h at March equinox and 0h at September equinox.

To understand when we should expect the extrema to occur, we need to move into the complex 3D world. GD is a bright star with RA ≈ 18h and Dec ≈ 51.5°, which conveniently passes nearly overhead of London (latitude≈51.5∘\approx 51.5^\circ≈51.5∘) and suffers minimal atmospheric distortion. In June and December, the Earth is at the closest and farthest points relative to GD, respectively, and thus the extrema are expected to occur in these two months. Starting from December 1725, Bradley and several other astronomers started to replicate Hooke's experiment. But to their surprise, the extrema of declination occurred in March and September.

The Corpuscular Explanation of Aberration

A few years later, Bradley managed to consolidate the results and explained the findings with Newton’s corpuscular theory of light, which assumes that light consists of particles traveling at speed ccc in a fixed medium. For an observer moving at velocity vvv, in their rest frame, the light appears to tilt into the observer's direction of motion. Thus, they must tilt their telescope by an aberration angle θ\thetaθ so that the light enters despite the sideways motion of the telescope.

2. The Rise of the Wave Theory

Note to time travelers. In the late 19th century, Maxwell’s equations were often written in individual components. We will use the compact vector form for clarity, but keep in mind that contemporaries might be unfamiliar with this notation.

By the early 19th century, overwhelming evidence supported the wave theory of light—where light propagated as oscillations in a luminiferous ether. Young’s double-slit experiment had revealed interference, and Fresnel’s work explained diffraction with remarkable accuracy.

3. The "Dragged" Ether

The Wave Explanation of Aberration

Explaining stellar aberration with wave theory was less straightforward. If light is treated as a plane wave arriving from a star, its direction should be unaffected by Earth’s motion. However, astronomers still observed aberration.

(Left) Particle Theory. Light ray appears tilted according to the particle model. (Right) Wave Theory. The plane wave looks the same both frames. (Figure adapted from mathpages.)

Theorists explained this by considering the telescope’s geometry. To match the phase of two waves at the focal point, when the Earth moves sideways at velocity vvv, the light must enter at a small angle θ\thetaθ so that the phase matches at the midpoint of the telescope.

Partial Ether Dragging Hypothesis

But this explanation has an implication: filling the telescope with a medium (e.g., water) should change θ\thetaθ, since light’s speed in a medium differs from ccc. Specifically, if the medium has refractive index nnn, light travels at speed c/nc/nc/n in the medium, so the angle in the telescope needs to be v/(c/n)=nv/cv/(c/n) = nv/cv/(c/n)=nv/c. Further, when light enters the medium from air, the angle is reduced by 1/n1/n1/n, so the aberration angle is expected to be n2v/cn^2v/cn2v/c.

Fresnel's hypothesis suggested that due to the partial dragging effect, to the first-order of v/cv/cv/c, no experiments can detect the relative motion between the ether and the Earth.

4. The Undetectable Ether

Michelson and Morley

By the late 19th century, Fresnel's partial ether-dragging and Lorentz's local time ideas had explained all first-order (v/c)(v/c)(v/c) optical experiments. But if Earth is moving through a stationary ether, there should still be a measurable effect at second order in v/cv/cv/c. Michelson and Morley (1887) set out to detect this: a change in the speed of light depending on the direction of Earth's motion through the ether.

They built an interferometer with two perpendicular arms of length LLL, mounted on a massive stone slab floating in mercury so the whole device could rotate smoothly. A beam splitter sent light down both arms; reflected beams recombined to form an interference pattern. Due to the relative motion between the Earth and the ether, the travel times in each arm would differ, causing a difference in phase and an observable fringe shift.

5. Einstein's Intuition

The Asymmetry in Electrodynamics

No one will illuminate the intuition better than Einstein himself:

It is known that Maxwell’s electrodynamics—as usually understood at the present time—when applied to moving bodies, leads to asymmetries which do not appear to be inherent in the phenomena. Take, for example, the reciprocal electrodynamic action of a magnet and a conductor. The observable phenomenon here depends only on the relative motion of the conductor and the magnet, whereas the customary view draws a sharp distinction between the two cases in which either the one or the other of these bodies is in motion. For if the magnet is in motion and the conductor at rest, there arises in the neighbourhood of the magnet an electric field with a certain definite energy, producing a current at the places where parts of the conductor are situated. But if the magnet is stationary and the conductor in motion, no electric field arises in the neighbourhood of the magnet. In the conductor, however, we find an electromotive force, to which in itself there is no corresponding energy, but which gives rise—assuming equality of relative motion in the two cases discussed—to electric currents of the same path and intensity as those produced by the electric forces in the former case. (Einstein, 1905.)

The Useless Ether

A remarkable fact is that Einstein didn't refer to Michelson & Morley's experiments or other second-order experiments that report null results in detecting ether wind in his 1905 paper. Instead, he cited the null results of first-order experiments. However, he disregarded Fresnel's partial dragging theory, and interpreted the null results as the proof that "to the first order of small quantities, the same laws of electrodynamics and optics will be valid for all frames of reference for which the equations of mechanics hold good", or the relativity principle. Fundamentally, Einstein discards ether or any absolute space as the reference frame.

The Relativity of Simultaneity

The Transformation between Inertial Frames is Linear. There are several natural assumptions about the transformation TTT, such as one-to-one and invertibility. Another important assumption is the homogeneity of space and time, or no preferred origin in space or time. Intuitively, this means the laws of physics are the same everywhere and everywhen. This implies the additivity of the transformation:

T(x+a,t+b)=T(x,t)+T(a,b)T(x+a, t+b) = T(x, t) + T(a, b)T(x+a,t+b)=T(x,t)+T(a,b)

If we further assume the continuity of the transformation, we have that TTT is linear. For example,

TGalilean=(1−v01)T_{\text{Galilean}} = \begin{pmatrix}1&-v\\0&1\end{pmatrix}TGalilean​=(10​−v1​)
TLorentz=(γ−γv−γvx/c2γ)T_{\text{Lorentz}} = \begin{pmatrix}\gamma&-\gamma v\\-\gamma vx/{c^2}&\gamma\end{pmatrix}TLorentz​=(γ−γvx/c2​−γvγ​)

Lorentz's "Local time" is probably equally susceptible to Einstein. Why would nature tolerate a pure mathematical trick without any physical significance? Space and time coordinates are for specific physical events in an inertial frame. The inertial frames can have different origins, but we can find the transformation between them. Let's consider space-time transformation of a 1D space:

(x′,t′)=T(x,t)(x', t') = T(x, t)(x′,t′)=T(x,t)

where (x, t) is the coordinate of an event in an inertial frame SSS, and (x', t') is the coordinate of the same event in another inertial frame S′S'S′. The most intuitive transformation to us is the Galilean transformation: if a frame S' is moving at speed vvv relative to another frame S, and if we synchronize the clocks of S and S' (set t=t′=0t=t'=0t=t′=0), then

(x′,t′)=(x−vt,t)(x',t') = (x-vt, t)(x′,t′)=(x−vt,t)

Even if we don't synchronize the clocks, we will expect the time to go by the same rate, and hence always differ by a constant. A consequence of this intuition is that, if two events are simultaneous in frame SSS (happen at the same time ttt), then they are also simultaneous in frame S′S'S′ (happen at the same time t′t't′).

That's why we might feel uneasy to assign a physical meaning to Lorentz transformation: it suggests time is going at different rates in different frames, and that simultaneity is relative!
